BACKGROUND
A  regional  logistics  and  supply  chain  provider  has  recently  expanded  its  operations  to  support  multiple
warehouse  facilities  across  the  country.  As  part  of  this  expansion,  the  organisation  plans  to  deploy  a
centralised  Warehouse  Operations  Management  System  that  enables  warehouse  personnel  to  manage
shipment tracking, inventory updates, delivery scheduling, operational reporting, and supplier coordination
through a web-based platform.
During an internal operational assessment, the organisation identified that its existing IT environment is
heavily  decentralised,  inconsistently  configured,  and  lacks  adequate  security  visibility  and  operational
resilience required to support business-critical logistics services.
The organisation therefore requires a secure and operational infrastructure capable of supporting the new
platform while ensuring availability, integrity of operational data, accountability of administrative actions, and
recoverability during service disruptions.
Your organisation has been engaged as a cyber engineering consultancy to design and implement the
required  environment.  You  have  been  assigned  as  the  lead  Cyber  Engineering  Analyst  responsible  for
delivering a production-ready solution.

Engineering-First Philosophy
Unlike traditional cybersecurity competitions, no infrastructure, software products, deployment guides,
templates,  or  predefined  architectures  will  be  provided.  Participants  are  expected  to  independently
determine  the  most  appropriate  technologies,  deployment  methods,  operational  controls,  security
mechanisms, monitoring solutions, and recovery strategies based on practical engineering judgement
and industry-relevant practices.
The objective of this challenge is not to evaluate familiarity with any specific technology vendor or
platform, but rather to assess the participant's ability to independently design, deploy, secure, monitor,
troubleshoot,  document,  and  recover  a  functional  operational  environment  using  sound  cyber
engineering principles.
COMPETITION DURATION
Participants will be given three (3) weeks to complete the challenge.
During this period, participants may build and deploy their solution using any environment of their choosing.
This  may  include  cloud  infrastructure,  local  virtualisation  environments,  physical  hardware,  containerised
deployments, or hybrid approaches.
All  provisioning,  software  installation,  system  configuration,  operational  testing,  troubleshooting,  security
implementation,  documentation,  and  demonstration  preparation  activities  are  the  responsibility  of  the
participant.
PROJECT REQUIREMENTS
The logistics provider requires a publicly accessible web-based platform capable of supporting warehouse
and shipment management operations.
The  chosen  solution  must  be  deployed  on  an  operating  system  selected  by  the  participant  and  must
incorporate appropriate security controls to protect both operational services and stored business data.
The deployed environment must demonstrate secure administrative access controls and appropriate user
management practices.
Participants are expected to implement measures that reduce the likelihood of unauthorised access while
maintaining operational usability for warehouse administrators and authorised personnel.
The solution should demonstrate evidence of secure baseline configuration practices and appropriate system
hardening measures for production-facing infrastructure.
The  application  environment  must  include  a  database  component  capable  of  storing  inventory  records,
shipment details, operational logs, and user account information.

Participants  are  expected  to  implement  suitable  database  protection  mechanisms  and  demonstrate  an
understanding  of  least-privilege  access  principles,  secure  credential  management,  service  isolation,  and
secure configuration practices.
The organisation has also identified a requirement for operational visibility into system and security events.
Participants are therefore required to implement monitoring, logging, or observability capabilities that allow
administrators to identify system activity, user actions, operational issues, and potentially security-relevant
events.
The technologies selected to fulfil these requirements are entirely at the discretion of the participant.
In addition, the organisation requires assurance that critical services and operational data can be restored
following  accidental  deletion,  hardware  failure,  service  interruption,  ransomware  simulation,  or  other
operational incidents.
Participants must therefore design and implement an appropriate backup and recovery approach capable of
restoring business-critical functionality and data integrity.
Participants are encouraged to prioritise maintainability, operational simplicity, service reliability, and practical
recovery procedures throughout their implementation.
DELIVERABLES
At the conclusion of the challenge, participants shall submit a technical package containing sufficient evidence
to demonstrate the design, implementation, security, operation, monitoring, and recoverability of their solution.
The submission should include:
Solution architecture diagram
Technical implementation documentation
Security configuration evidence
Administrative access control documentation
Monitoring and logging evidence
Backup and recovery procedures
Operational validation or testing evidence
Supporting artefacts relevant to the solution
Evidence may include screenshots, configuration files, logs, command outputs, deployment notes, validation
results, scripts, diagrams, or any additional material considered relevant by the participant.
Participants should assume that assessors have not previously seen their environment.
Documentation should therefore be sufficiently detailed to allow assessors to understand the participant's
design decisions, implementation approach, operational considerations, and applied security controls.

LIVE DEMONSTRATION
Each participant or team will be allocated thirty (30) minutes for a live demonstration and technical defence
session.
During this session, participants will be expected to present their architecture and explain the rationale behind
their technology selections, deployment decisions, security controls, monitoring implementation, and recovery
strategy.
Participants must demonstrate that their solution is operational and capable of supporting the stated business
requirements.
Assessors may request demonstrations involving:
Administrative access management
Security control implementation
Database protection mechanisms
Monitoring and alerting capabilities
Logging visibility
Backup validation
Recovery procedures
Operational troubleshooting
The demonstration should also provide evidence that participants understand the engineering decisions they
have made.
Assessors  may  ask  participants  to  discuss  alternative  approaches,  explain  trade-offs,  justify  technology
selections, and describe how operational and security risks were mitigated.
Participants should be prepared to defend both their technical implementation and the reasoning behind their
engineering decisions.
ASSESSMENT PHILOSOPHY
The JURUS programme adopts an engineering-first assessment philosophy.
Assessment will focus on demonstrated practical capability rather than theoretical knowledge alone.
Participants will be evaluated based on:
Quality and completeness of implementation
Effectiveness of security controls
Operational maintainability and usability
Clarity and completeness of documentation
Visibility provided through monitoring and logging

A more complex solution will not necessarily receive a higher score. Greater emphasis will be placed on
solutions that are appropriate, secure, maintainable, operationally practical, well-documented, and clearly
understood by the participant.
The highest-scoring submissions will demonstrate not only technical competence, but also strong engineering
judgement, structured operational thinking, and professional communication skills.
EXPECTED OUTCOME
Upon completion of the challenge, successful participants should demonstrate the capability to independently
design, build, secure, monitor, troubleshoot, document, and recover a production-ready operational system
using industry-relevant cyber engineering practices.
This outcome aligns directly with the JURUS Level 1 Analyst competency objective of developing practitioners
capable  of  independently  building  and  securing  systems  while  applying  structured  troubleshooting
methodologies, operational discipline, and sound engineering judgement.
